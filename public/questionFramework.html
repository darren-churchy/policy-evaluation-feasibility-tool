Part Two

Quantitative Research Questions

Three question types forming a hierarchy of claim strength. Each stronger type requires a stronger identification strategy to support it. Causal inference has two distinct applications — mechanism/efficacy testing at the Test stage and effectiveness evaluation at the Grow stage — but both require the same standard of identification.

⬧

Boundary case — "drivers" and "risk factor" questions

These are among the most frequently misclassified question types. The language implies causation but the methods used typically support only associational claims. Placement depends on what is being claimed and whether an identification strategy exists.

→ Prediction
"Which factors are associated with reoffending?"
Regression, descriptive modelling. Reports association. The word "driver" exceeds what the method supports.
→ Causal Inference
"Does housing instability cause reoffending?"
Requires a credible identification strategy. Most "risk factor" analyses do not meet this bar.

Question Types Across the Test and Learn Cycle

The Test and Learn framework organises evaluation activity around four stages: Explore, Co-design, Test, and Grow. Different question types belong at different stages. This table shows where qualitative and quantitative question types are most naturally located — and what each stage is trying to learn.

Explore
What is the system? What is the problem?
Primary question types
Systems & Complexity Contextual Diagnostic Description
Analytical purpose
Build shared understanding of the system. Map actors, relationships, feedback loops, and behavioural drivers. Identify leverage points. Surface assumptions and evidence gaps. Establish baseline patterns.
Typical methods
Systems mapping · Actor mapping · SSM · Stakeholder engagement · Rapid evidence review · Descriptive data analysis · Social network analysis · Behavioural diagnosis
Outputs: shared system map, behavioural analysis, evidence gaps, leverage point assessment
Co-design
What might work? What do people need?
Primary question types
Strategic Systems & Complexity Contextual Description Prediction
Analytical purpose
Generate and compare intervention options. Build a Theory of Change. Test paper prototypes with users and staff. Narrow to most promising designs before investment. Refine ToC assumptions.
Typical methods
Co-production workshops · Prototype testing · Observation & shadowing · Think-aloud testing · Journey mapping · Short interviews · Quick surveys · Theory of Change development
Outputs: prioritised prototypes, initial Theory of Change, refined assumptions, decision on which designs to test
Test
Are the critical assumptions holding?
Primary question types
Diagnostic Evaluative — Process Evaluative — Impact Prediction Causal — Efficacy
Analytical purpose
Test riskiest assumptions in real-world settings. Examine whether mechanisms are operating as expected. Assess early behavioural signals and short-term outcomes. Identify unintended consequences. Inform iteration decisions.
Typical methods
Qualitative sprints · A/B testing · Nimble RCTs · Mechanism experiments · Efficacy trials · Bayesian adaptive trials · Process evaluation techniques · Contribution stories (QUIP) · Proxy/mechanism-level measures
Outputs: evidence on critical assumptions, updated ToC, decision to iterate/continue/stop
Grow
Does it work at scale, for whom, at what cost?
Primary question types
Evaluative — Process Evaluative — Impact Causal — Effectiveness
Analytical purpose
Robustly evaluate a stable, well-specified intervention. Establish whether it achieves intended outcomes, for whom, and at what cost. Determine whether to scale, adapt, or stop. Inform decisions about place-based models.
Typical methods
RCT · DiD · RDD · ITS · IV · Matching/PSM · Realist evaluation · Contribution analysis · Process evaluation · Value for money analysis
Outputs: impact findings, process evaluation, VfM assessment, scale/adapt/stop recommendation
⚠ On causal inference across stages: The identification standard for causal questions does not relax at the Test stage. A nimble RCT, A/B test, or mechanism experiment still requires a valid identification strategy — what changes is the estimand (a specific component or mechanism rather than whole-programme effectiveness) and the decision it informs (iterate vs scale). Both Causal — Efficacy and Causal — Effectiveness require explicit identification assumptions, documented and tested where possible.
